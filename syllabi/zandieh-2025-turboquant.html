Syllabus — TurboQuant and the rotation-based quantization line

Summary

TurboQuant (Zandieh, Daliri, Hadian, Mirrokni — Google Research, ICLR 2026) is an online, data-oblivious vector quantization algorithm that achieves near-optimal rate-distortion for both MSE and inner-product reconstruction of high-dimensional Euclidean vectors. The construction is a two-stage pipeline: (1) randomly rotate the input so each coordinate becomes Beta(½, (d-1)/2)-distributed and near-independent, then apply a precomputed Lloyd-Max scalar quantizer per coordinate at b bits; (2) apply a 1-bit QJL (Quantized Johnson-Lindenstrauss) transform to the residual to remove the multiplicative bias that MSE-optimal quantizers introduce in inner-product estimation. The data-oblivious property — no codebook training, no per-block scales — makes it suitable for streaming KV-cache compression and online ANN indexing. The paper reports parity with full-precision LongBench scores at 3.5 bits/channel on Llama-3.1-8B and ~99% Recall@10 on 1536-dim OpenAI embeddings with effectively zero indexing time (vs ~240s for PQ, ~2270s for RaBitQ on the same benchmark).

Key points

Two-stage construction

Given a unit-norm input $x \in \mathbb{R}^d$: 1. MSE stage — rotate $x \leftarrow \Pi x$ with a uniformly random orthogonal $\Pi$, then per-coordinate Lloyd-Max scalar quantize to $\tilde{x}_{\text{mse}}$ at $b-1$ bits. 2. QJL residual stage — compute $r = x - \tilde{x}_{\text{mse}}$, project via a random sign matrix $S$, store $\text{sign}(Sr)$ as 1 bit per output dimension, plus $\gamma = \|r\|_2$. 3. Decode — $\hat{x} = \tilde{x}_{\text{mse}} + \tilde{x}_{\text{qjl}}$ where $\tilde{x}_{\text{qjl}} = \frac{\pi/2}{d} \gamma S^T \text{sign}(Sr)$.

Distortion bound: $D_{\text{prod}} \leq \frac{\sqrt{3}\pi^2 \|y\|_2^2}{d \cdot 4^b}$ — order-optimal in $b$.

Why random rotation matters

After uniform random rotation, coordinates of a unit-norm vector are marginally Beta-distributed: $f_X(x) = \frac{\Gamma(d/2)}{\sqrt{\pi}\Gamma((d-1)/2)}(1-x^2)^{(d-3)/2}$ on $[-1,1]$, converging to $\mathcal{N}(0, 1/d)$. Distinct coordinates become near-independent in high $d$ — this is what licenses per-coordinate scalar quantization without coding losses. Precomputed Lloyd-Max codebooks for this Beta distribution: $b=1 \Rightarrow c \approx \{\pm\sqrt{2/(\pi d)}\}$; $b=2 \Rightarrow c \approx \{\pm 0.453/\sqrt{d}, \pm 1.51/\sqrt{d}\}$. Codebooks are universal — computed once, never adapted.

Why the QJL residual

A pure MSE-optimal scalar quantizer is biased for inner-product estimation: e.g., the 1-bit MSE quantizer has multiplicative bias $2/\pi$. ANN search and attention both need accurate inner products, not just small MSE. The 1-bit QJL projection on the residual is unbiased — $\mathbb{E}[\langle y, \tilde{x}_{\text{qjl}}\rangle] = \langle y, r\rangle$ — so the combined estimator $\langle y, \hat{x}\rangle$ is unbiased while inheriting near-optimal variance from the small residual norm.

Data-oblivious / online

No data-dependent training. The random rotation is a fixed random matrix (or simulated via structured rotations like Hadamard), the codebooks are precomputed from the Beta distribution, and there are no per-block scale/zero-point metadata to store. This is what makes TurboQuant fit KV cache compression during autoregressive generation, where new tokens arrive one at a time with unknown statistics, and online ANN insertion.

Headline results

KV cache (Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, LongBench):

Config Bits/channel Avg score vs full FP16
Full precision 16 50.06 —
TurboQuant 3.5 50.06 0.00
TurboQuant 2.5 49.44 −0.62

Mixed-precision strategy gives 2.5 b/channel average: 32 outlier channels at 3 bits + 96 regular channels at 2 bits. Needle-in-a-Haystack stays at 0.997 recall at 4× compression.

ANN search (DBpedia OpenAI 1536-dim):

Method Index time Recall@10
PQ 239.75 s ~92%
RaBitQ 2267.59 s ~88%
TurboQuant (4-bit) 0.0013 s ~98%

Indexing collapses to almost free because there’s nothing to train.

What’s not addressed

  • The paper focuses on Euclidean / inner-product geometry. Non-Euclidean distances (e.g., Manhattan, cosine on non-normalized data) are out of scope.
  • The QJL residual stage adds $O(d)$ bits per vector for the sign portion plus storage for $\gamma$ — this is a fixed cost that becomes proportionally more expensive at very low bit widths.
  • Comparison with newer rotation-based LLM weight/activation quantizers (QuaRot, SpinQuant) is implicit; TurboQuant targets KV cache and ANN, those target weights.

Random rotation in high dimensions

Intuition

In high dimensions, a uniformly random orthogonal rotation makes any unit vector look “generic” — its coordinates concentrate near zero, become marginally identically distributed, and become near-independent. This single trick is the foundation under both the Johnson–Lindenstrauss lemma (used in JL projections, locality-sensitive hashing) and modern rotation-then-quantize methods (RaBitQ, TurboQuant, QJL, QuaRot, SpinQuant).

The mental model: a random rotation is like spreading the “interesting” information of a vector equally across all coordinates. Whatever the original signal structure was — a few large outlier channels, a sparse representation, a clustering — rotation flattens it.

The math (1 paragraph)

Let $x$ be a fixed unit vector in $\mathbb{R}^d$ and $\Pi$ a uniform random orthogonal matrix. Then each coordinate of $y = \Pi x$ has marginal density

$$f_Y(t) = \frac{\Gamma(d/2)}{\sqrt{\pi}\,\Gamma((d-1)/2)} (1 - t^2)^{(d-3)/2}, \quad t \in [-1, 1]$$

— a Beta$(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{d-1}{2})$-like distribution. As $d \to \infty$, $\sqrt{d}\, y_i \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$, and any finite subset of coordinates becomes asymptotically independent. The deviations are $O(1/\sqrt{d})$ in total variation.

Johnson–Lindenstrauss in two lines

For $n$ points in $\mathbb{R}^d$ and $\epsilon > 0$, a random projection to $m = O(\epsilon^{-2} \log n)$ dimensions preserves all pairwise distances within a $(1\pm\epsilon)$ factor with high probability. The projection is just a Gaussian (or random-sign, or random-Hadamard) matrix. This is what licenses random projection as a data-oblivious dimensionality reduction.

Worked example: 1-bit quantization on the sphere

Sample $x$ uniformly on $S^{d-1}$ and quantize to $\text{sign}(x) \in \{\pm 1\}^d$. The reconstructed unit vector is $\sqrt{1/d} \, \text{sign}(x)$.

Expected inner product with original:

$$\mathbb{E}\langle x, \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{d}}\text{sign}(x)\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{d}} \sum_i \mathbb{E}|x_i| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{d}} \cdot d \cdot \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi d}} = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} \approx 0.798$$

That $\sqrt{2/\pi}$ factor is the bias every 1-bit MSE-optimal sign quantizer carries — it shows up in RaBitQ as the ratio normalization, and in QJL/TurboQuant as the $\pi/2$ scale in the unbiased estimator.

Practical: structured rotations

A dense random orthogonal multiply costs $O(d^2)$ which is prohibitive for $d \sim 4096$. In practice everyone uses structured rotations:

  • Random Hadamard / Walsh-Hadamard: $\Pi = H D$ where $H$ is the Hadamard matrix and $D$ is a random ±1 diagonal. Cost $O(d \log d)$, preserves JL-like properties.
  • Block-Hadamard + permutation: used in QuaRot / SpinQuant for LLM weight quantization.
  • Random rotation cached in model weights: bake the rotation into adjacent linear layers so it’s free at inference time.

Why this matters for quantization

A unit vector with arbitrary coordinate distribution is hard to quantize — outlier coordinates blow up the dynamic range. A rotated unit vector is well-behaved: coordinates are bounded $O(1/\sqrt{d})$, distributed identically, near-independent. Lloyd-Max scalar quantization on the Beta marginal is then near-Shannon-optimal, without ever looking at the data. This is the kernel of TurboQuant.

Vector quantization and rate-distortion

Intuition

Quantization replaces a continuous signal with a finite set of code points so it can be stored in $b$ bits per sample. Two regimes:

  • Scalar quantization — each coordinate is rounded independently to one of $2^b$ levels. Optimal level placement for a fixed marginal distribution is given by the Lloyd-Max algorithm (essentially 1-D k-means).
  • Vector quantization (VQ) — the whole vector is mapped to one of $2^{bd}$ codepoints chosen to cover the support of the joint distribution. Theoretically wins because it can exploit correlations between coordinates; practically wins only when the codebook is trainable and small enough to search.

The Shannon rate-distortion theorem gives a lower bound on the MSE distortion any encoder with rate $R$ bits per sample can achieve. For an i.i.d. Gaussian source with variance $\sigma^2$: $D(R) = \sigma^2 \cdot 2^{-2R}$ — the famous “$6$ dB per bit” rule. Real quantizers are within constants of this bound; rotation-based methods (RaBitQ, TurboQuant) achieve those constants without training.

Lloyd-Max scalar quantizer

For a 1-D source with density $f(x)$, find $b$ thresholds $t_1 < \cdots < t_{2^b - 1}$ and $2^b$ centroids $c_i$ minimizing

$$\sum_{i=1}^{2^b} \int_{t_{i-1}}^{t_i} (x - c_i)^2 f(x)\, dx$$

The optimality conditions are: thresholds at midpoints of consecutive centroids, centroids at conditional means. Solve by fixed-point iteration. For Gaussian / Beta / Laplacian sources the optima are tabulated and reusable — no per-instance training needed.

Why VQ usually doesn’t help in practice

The codebook size grows as $2^{bd}$, so VQ for $d=128$, $b=2$ has $2^{256}$ codepoints — impossible to enumerate. Workarounds: - Product quantization (Product Quantization (Jegou et al., 2011)): break the vector into low-d chunks and VQ each. Loses cross-subspace correlations. - Tree-structured VQ / lattice VQ: structured codebooks searchable in $O(d)$. - Rotation + scalar VQ (the modern winner — RaBitQ, TurboQuant): randomly rotate to make coordinates near-independent, then scalar-quantize per coordinate. Recovers VQ-like distortion at scalar-quantization cost.

Why rotation rescues scalar quantization

Independence of coordinates means the joint distortion is the sum of per-coordinate distortions — no cross-coordinate gain a VQ codebook could have captured. Random rotation of an arbitrary unit vector produces marginals that converge to $\mathcal{N}(0, 1/d)$ with vanishing dependence (see Random rotation in high dimensions). Scalar Lloyd-Max on those marginals is then near-optimal — and data-oblivious, since the marginal distribution only depends on $d$.

KIVI (Liu et al., 2024)

researchSource PDF: liu-2024-kivi.pdf
Part of
Syllabus — TurboQuant and the rotation-based quantization line

Summary

KIVI (Liu et al., 2024) is a tuning-free 2-bit quantization scheme for the KV cache of decoder-only LLMs that exploits the asymmetric outlier structure of keys vs values: keys are quantized per-channel (group along the feature axis), values are quantized per-token (group along the sequence axis). The asymmetry comes from an empirical observation — in attention K, a few feature channels carry persistent large-magnitude outliers, whereas in V the outliers are scattered token-wise. Quantizing each in its “natural” direction keeps INT2 representations within the dynamic range of the dominant signal without sacrificing accuracy. Reported gains: 2.6× peak memory reduction, 2.35–3.47× decode throughput, up to 4× larger serving batch size, with quality parity vs FP16 across Llama, Falcon, Mistral. KIVI represents the data-dependent KV-quantization line that TurboQuant and QJL deliberately step away from.

Key points

The outlier asymmetry

  • Keys: a small set of channels (~3–5%) consistently has activations ~10× larger than the rest, across all tokens. Per-channel quantization isolates these channels with their own scale, leaving the rest in tight range.
  • Values: outliers are per-token (specific tokens like punctuation or BOS have larger activations across all channels). Per-token quantization gives each token its own scale.

This is the same pattern Dettmers et al. observed for LLM weights (the basis of LLM.int8); KIVI is the KV-cache version.

Algorithm

For each block of new KV pairs: - K is reshaped to (channel, group_size) and quantized to INT2 per channel with a per-channel scale + zero-point. - V is reshaped to (token, hidden) and quantized to INT2 per token with a per-token scale + zero-point. - A small residual buffer (~last 128 tokens) is kept in FP16 to avoid quantization noise on the most attention-relevant tokens. - During attention, dequantize on the fly into a tile of the right shape before matmul.

Tuning-free

No calibration pass, no per-model hyperparameters. Quantization parameters are derived directly from the running block’s min/max. This is what makes KIVI cheap to ship — you don’t need a held-out dataset, unlike GPTQ-style methods.

Benchmark headline

Llama-2-7B / Llama-2-13B / Falcon-7B / Mistral-7B across LongBench: - INT2 KIVI quality ≈ FP16 quality (deltas <0.5 point on most tasks) - Peak memory: 2.6× smaller (including model weights — the KV cache fraction shrinks 8×) - Decode throughput: 2.35× (small batches) to 3.47× (large batches) - Max batch size: up to 4× vs FP16 baseline at the same GPU memory budget

Limitations relative to TurboQuant / QJL

  • The per-channel / per-token scales are metadata stored at FP16 → real effective bits per KV value are ~2.5–3 bits, not 2.
  • Tuning-free in the sense of “no calibration”, but still data-dependent (statistics are computed from each block).
  • The residual FP16 buffer is essential for accuracy — pure INT2 without the residual degrades non-trivially.

These are exactly the costs the data-oblivious approach (QJL, TurboQuant) is designed to eliminate.

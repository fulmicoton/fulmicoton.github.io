Syllabus — TurboQuant and the rotation-based quantization line

What this covers

TurboQuant (Zandieh et al., ICLR 2026) is the current state of the art for data-oblivious vector quantization — quantize a high-dimensional vector to a few bits per coordinate, with no training, no per-block scales, and near-Shannon-rate distortion. It matters because it makes extreme KV-cache compression (≤3 bits/channel) and online ANN indexing essentially free.

The syllabus traces the lineage: classical product quantization → the theoretical breakthroughs of RaBitQ and QJL → TurboQuant as their unification → contrast with the data-dependent KV-cache line (KIVI). After reading this you should understand why “rotate then scalar-quantize” became the dominant recipe, what the two-stage MSE + QJL construction buys you over either alone, and what the open problems are below 2 bits/channel.

Prerequisites

  • Vector quantization and rate-distortion — Lloyd-Max scalar quantizers, Shannon’s rate-distortion bound, why VQ usually doesn’t beat scalar VQ in practice.
  • Random rotation in high dimensions — Johnson-Lindenstrauss, why a random rotation makes coordinates Beta-distributed and near-independent, structured Hadamard rotations.
  • MSE vs inner-product quantization — the $2/\pi$ bias of MSE-optimal sign quantizers, ratio normalization (RaBitQ), asymmetric estimators (QJL), two-stage residual fixing (TurboQuant).
  • KV cache quantization — what the KV cache is, why decode is memory-bound, the key/value outlier asymmetry.

Reading order

  1. Product Quantization (Jegou et al., 2011) — the classical baseline; understand what “code length” and “asymmetric distance computation” mean before reading anything modern. Read for the IVFADC architecture and to see what theoretical guarantees were missing.
  2. RaBitQ (Gao et al., 2024) — the breakthrough that proved you can do 1 bit/dim with an asymptotically optimal error bound, using rotation + sign. Read for the unbiased ratio estimator and the connection to Alon–Klartag’s lower bound.
  3. QJL (Zandieh et al., 2024) — same sign-after-rotation idea, but for the LLM KV-cache setting, with an asymmetric estimator that is the building block TurboQuant reuses. Read for the “zero overhead” argument and the unbiased inner-product estimator.
  4. KIVI (Liu et al., 2024) — the contrast: a data-dependent KV-cache quantization scheme that exploits the per-channel-K / per-token-V outlier asymmetry. Read to understand what TurboQuant deliberately gives up by going data-oblivious, and what it gets in return.
  5. TurboQuant (Zandieh et al., 2025) — finally, the synthesis. Lloyd-Max scalar quantization on the Beta marginal (MSE stage) + 1-bit QJL on the residual (bias fix). Generalizes RaBitQ to any bit-width and beats KIVI’s effective rate by removing all metadata.

Implementations

  • TurboQuant — implementation landscape — landscape: no official Google release, several community PyTorch/Triton/MLX/Rust implementations of varying fidelity, official QJL code for the building block.

Open questions

  • Sub-2-bit KV cache: every method falls off the quality cliff below 2 bits/channel. Is the cliff fundamental (Shannon-rate sets it) or is there a smarter construction (mixed-precision per-layer, attention-aware, sparse-and-dense)?
  • Rotation cost at inference: structured Hadamard rotations are $O(d \log d)$ but still measurable. Can the rotation be folded into adjacent linear layers without breaking the data-oblivious property?
  • Beyond Euclidean: TurboQuant is built around Euclidean / inner-product geometry. Cosine on non-normalized vectors, Mahalanobis, and learned metrics are not directly addressed.
  • Composition with KV-cache eviction: StreamingLLM, H2O, and similar attention-aware eviction policies are orthogonal in principle. Stacking them with TurboQuant is unvalidated.
  • Will Google release official code? Implementation choices (exact codebook, channel selection for mixed precision) currently vary across forks. A canonical reference would resolve fidelity arguments.
